## The anchor lines (each can be a near-empty slide)
1. **"Care should make you feel like you belong — not like you're special."**
2. **"People whose lives were taken don't yearn for the cure. They yearn to feel normal again."**
3. **"When you're the only one who seems to care how you feel, how do you trust anyone offering to help?"**
4. **"Not AI, or your doctor. AI *and* your doctor. We bridge the gap by reimagining the experience."**

## The threads (what the deck must carry)
- **Agency through the search.** You want help; what's offered isn't what you need; so you go searching alone. DOT
  gives the agency back — you tell your story, you draft your report, you drive.
- **Empathy, both ways.** Make it easier for *you* to be heard and felt, AND for the people who can help to
  understand you. DOT sits in the seam between patient and provider.
- **The hidden struggle.** You can watch someone struggle and never know — they hide it, because of what being seen
  would mean. We don't design for the person who's hiding.
- **Belong, not special.** Care is built for people who already feel they belong; it makes the ones who need it most
  feel marked. Reverse it.
- **Normal, not cured.** The yearning is to feel normal again — and that starts at the very first moment of contact.
- **The trust hole.** Platforms that look like health reinforce that you're a patient; if you feel alone in caring
  about yourself, you can't trust the resource. DOT doesn't look like health — it looks like being seen.
- **The bridge (the why-now + the safety).** The AI-told-someone-to-die cases happen because it's AI *or* providers.
  DOT is the *and* — designed against those failure modes (validate-first, never invalidate, human-on-risk).

## Refined problem statement (woven — draft for the deck)
> *When your life gets taken from you, you don't yearn for the cure. You yearn to feel normal again. But every door
> you're offered makes you feel the opposite — special, marked, different — when all you wanted was to belong. So
> you hide it. And when you finally reach for help, what's offered isn't what you needed, so you go searching alone,
> the only person who seems to care how you feel. That's the moment the system loses you. Care was never designed
> for the people who need it most. DOT is built for that gap — to make it easier for you to be heard, and for the
> people who can help to finally understand you. Not AI or your doctor. AI and your doctor. Because the cases where
> AI told someone to die should never happen — and they won't, if we reimagine the experience instead of choosing sides.*

## Opening story — the cold-email discovery (the pitch's first ~60 seconds)

**Verbatim seed (Johnny):** ~6 months ago, a school assignment to cold-email strangers to practice pitching. Treated
it the normal way — find their pain, their struggle, the cure they want. What he learned: they don't yearn for
medication/access — *they yearn to feel normal.* Care marks you / makes you different; when you reach for help it's
not what you needed, so you search alone, the only one who cares how you feel — *the stigma of personal care.* The
system fails us when we lack the confidence to know what's happening + the expertise to be diagnosed. *Care is only
as good as its diagnosis.* If Tom or Teri or anyone, any age, is too afraid to ask and too skeptical of AI — we
reimagined the future of care, and it keeps everyone safe (provider + user). → introduce DOT.

**Tightened (for the stage — preserves the voice):**
> *Six months ago, a school assignment had me cold-emailing strangers to practice pitching. So I did the thing you're
> taught to do: find their pain, their struggle, the cure they're chasing.*
>
> *That's not what I found. They didn't yearn for the medication, or the appointment, or the fix. They yearned to feel
> normal again.*
>
> *And that breaks the whole model. Because care, the way we built it, marks you — it makes you the different one. So
> when you finally reach for help and it isn't what you needed, you go searching alone — until you're the only person
> who seems to care how you feel. That's the real stigma of personal care.*
>
> *The system fails us exactly when we need it: we don't have the confidence to know what's happening to us, or the
> expertise to explain it well enough to be diagnosed. And care is only as good as its diagnosis. So if Tom, or Teri,
> or anyone — any age — is too afraid to ask and too skeptical to trust an AI, the whole thing breaks.*
>
> *So we reimagined care to keep everyone safe — the person and the provider.*
>
> *This is DOT. It gets to know your pain over time, and reflects back what's objective and what's subjective — so you
> can just speak, normally, and your provider can finally see what it takes to help.*

**Optional gut-punch** (drop right after "they yearned to feel normal again"): *"I already knew that. A therapist once
told me there was nothing wrong with me — because I'd learned to make it small."* → fuses the earned insight with the
lived truth; earns the room before the product.
